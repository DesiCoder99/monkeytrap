Bitcoin Value Debate: Proof-of-Work and Network Effects

Context Question

Why do people say Bitcoin is the greater fool's theory?

Original Comment

Because this has happened before, it's not too difficult to understand their position, all you have to do is to look at everything except well regulated developed country markets. Anything that comes with a lofty promise eventually ends up being a rug-pull. So they are on sound ground in rejecting something super intangible like Bitcoin, hell to them even tulip bulbs produced more tangible results.

The barrier that I personally had to overcome is understanding that the entirety of proof-of-work, all transactions are embedded in bitcoin-blockchain at-the-moment. This is cryptographically verifiable. Another realization needed is that the silicon & energy resources needed to perform that work is also embodied in current blockchain. It also represents the value of what could have been done using those resources, that is after all the value of anything. This value is real & it stands by itself.

So there is value embodied in Bitcoin itself. Now the question is how much? To that my answer is it's about network effects, current and past. It's not easy to replace the value formed organically in all players' minds by something that starting afresh. It's just not, otherwise we'll be creating new Google, Facebook & WhatsApp every year with better tech. And this is just current network effect. There is also past network effect embodied in bitcoin that I think is akin to Karma. This represents all the past interactions between current and (now) dormant players. This is like preserving the entire history of all currency transfers. You are not recreating that collective value that exists across all participants' minds anywhere else with a fresh new network no matter how hard you decree. Sure today all Central banks can issue their own CBDCs but even if they do that they still can't devalue the Bitcoin anymore. In fact (my hunch) is that likely more they try, more they'll end up validating ("Streisand"-ing?!) it and accelerating it. AFAIU the only real way to kill bitcoin was to choke it at on/off ramps to fiat when it was young. That fact that we are $2.4T cap now and that we were instrumental in swinging a major election (crypto-bros in swing states!) tells me that ship has long sailed.

So what's left, slow embrace by trailing currencies. I guess we are in early stages of that. So buckle-up!

Initial Critique

Strong Points

Proof-of-work insight: Correctly identifies that computational work and energy are irreversibly embedded in the blockchain. This is a legitimate value argument.

Network effects reasoning: Valid analogy to Google/Facebook—established networks have moat advantages. The distinction between current and historical network effects is thoughtful.

Weaknesses

1. Circular logic on value

"Silicon & energy resources... that is after all the value of anything" conflates cost with value. Sunk costs don't determine market value. If I burn $1000 of electricity mining a token nobody wants, that energy expenditure doesn't magically create $1000 of value.

2. "Past network effect" as karma

This is metaphysical hand-waving. Transaction history has archival value, but calling it "karma" that can't be recreated is mysticism dressed as economics. Ethereum forked and retained value. Networks can migrate.

3. Election impact overclaimed

Crypto's electoral impact was marginal and heavily debated. Crypto donors spent $119M, but whether crypto voters were decisive is disputed. Claiming "we were instrumental" overstates evidence.

4. CBDC strawman

"CBDCs can't devalue Bitcoin anymore" misunderstands the threat. CBDCs could reduce Bitcoin's utility as payment rail, not by "devaluing" it cryptographically but by offering superior UX/integration with zero volatility.

5. Inevitability fallacy

"Ship has sailed" + "slow embrace by trailing currencies" assumes linear adoption. $4T market cap ≠ inevitable global reserve currency status. MySpace hit $12B valuation before Facebook. Market cap is not destiny.

6. Missing counterarguments

  • Energy cost as liability, not pure asset
  • Regulatory risk beyond on/off ramps
  • Deflationary design problems for currency use
  • Concentration risk (whales own disproportionate supply)

Verdict

Decent intuition on computational work and network effects, but reasoning jumps from "has some embedded value" to "inevitable global currency" without rigorous support. Overconfident given actual election data and regulatory uncertainty.

Counter-Analysis

The Gold Analogy Works... and Reveals the Problem

You're correct: Gold's value does partially derive from extraction costs creating scarcity. The energy/labor embedded in mining gold contributes to its value proposition—you can't trivially create more.

Where it breaks down:

1. Gold has non-monetary intrinsic demand

  • Industrial uses (electronics, dentistry): ~10% of demand
  • Jewelry/aesthetic appeal: ~50% of demand
  • These create a value floor independent of monetary belief

Bitcoin has zero non-monetary demand. If collective belief evaporates, value → 0. Gold would still trade for industrial/aesthetic uses.

2. Marginal cost ≠ market price

Gold currently trades at ~2x production cost. Bitcoin's mining cost follows price (difficulty adjusts), not vice versa. When BTC crashes, miners capitulate and difficulty drops—the "embedded energy" thesis reverses causality.

3. "You can hold Bitcoin cryptographically"

This is linguistic sleight-of-hand. You hold private keys that prove control over ledger entries. Gold is a physical element; Bitcoin is a social consensus about which database entries are valid. Both require collective agreement to have value, but gold has a fallback.

Corrected Position

Embedded energy creates scarcity, not value. Scarcity + demand = value. Gold has demand beyond money. Bitcoin's only demand is as money/speculation. This makes the energy argument weaker, not stronger—you've just described an expensive way to create artificial scarcity without underlying utility.

The valid argument isn't "energy = value" but rather "provable scarcity + network effects + Schelling point = monetary premium." The energy is just the mechanism, not the value itself.

1. International monetary gold ≠ private gold holdings

2. Tragedy of commons analogy is backwards

Tragedy of commons = overexploitation of shared resource (fishing, grazing). Bitcoin's challenge is opposite: underutilization as actual currency due to hoarding (Gresham's Law—"good money drives out bad"). High MVRV shows people hold for appreciation, not transaction use.

This undermines the "it's money" thesis. It's becoming "digital gold" (store of value) not "digital cash."

3. Moon gold scenario

If we found massive lunar gold, industrial/jewelry demand would provide a partial floor (~$500-800/oz instead of $2700). Bitcoin has no such floor—it goes to cost of attack (~electricity for 51%) or zero.

Refined Position

You've convinced me the "energy = value" argument is stronger than I initially credited—but it's actually an argument that Bitcoin is becoming digital gold, not replacing fiat currency. The realized price metrics + improved distribution + diminishing speculation thesis all point toward:

  • Store of value ✓ (your argument holds)
  • Medium of exchange ✗ (MVRV shows hoarding)
  • Unit of account ✗ (volatility)

So the original comment's "slow embrace by trailing currencies" framing is still wrong. More likely: Bitcoin as parallel reserve asset, not currency replacement. That's still significant, but different from the victory lap implied.

Counter-Analysis - Meeting Halfway on Energy

Energy Cost Creates Value - Let's Drill Down

You're distinguishing historical energy (sunk) vs ongoing energy (security). I think I can meet you halfway:

Historical Energy Embedded in Blockchain

Your argument: Past PoW represents irreversible commitment of resources (energy/silicon/opportunity cost). This is embodied in current blockchain state and cannot be replicated without re-spending those resources.

I'll concede: This creates path dependency value. Similar to:

  • Gold's existing stock (175K tonnes mined over millennia)
  • Established brand equity (Coca-Cola's 100+ years of marketing spend)
  • Network effects (Facebook's social graph)

The key insight: You can't shortcut to Bitcoin's current state. Even if you launch "Bitcoin 2.0" with identical code, you don't get:

  • 15 years of battle-tested security
  • Proven Lindy effect (survived Mt. Gox, China bans, etc.)
  • Embedded transaction history (the "karma" you mentioned)

But here's the precise mechanism: The value isn't in the energy itself, it's in the demonstrated commitment + time-tested resilience that energy expenditure proves. The PoW is a costly signal of genuine belief/investment.

Where This Matters

This makes Bitcoin hard to replace, but doesn't determine price level. Analogy:

  • Gold: Historical mining effort → current 200K tonne stock → scarcity + industrial demand → ~$15T market cap
  • Bitcoin: Historical PoW → current 19.6M coin supply → scarcity + network effects → ~$2T market cap

The energy establishes credibility of scarcity, not value magnitude. Market determines latter.

Strong point. A civilization that diverts too much to gold mining vs. military/agriculture pays opportunity cost. Same applies to Bitcoin mining—it competes with AI compute, which is why:

This actually weakens the "productive opportunity cost" argument—miners specifically choose stranded/cheap energy. Bitcoin critics are right that mining doesn't displace high-value uses.
